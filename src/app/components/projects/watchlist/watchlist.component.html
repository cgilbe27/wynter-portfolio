Watchlist:

Designing an end-to-end application

Overview:

With the increasing amount of streaming services, it is hard to keep track of all the new movies and shows coming out. This surge in new streaming services media titles, people are also finding more difficulty in choosing what to watch next. The majority of people rely on family and friends’ recommendations. So, what if there was an app that let people catalog their watchlist and share their recommendations with friends?

The problem:

People are having difficulty keeping track of their media consumption as well as what they should watch next. There is also a need for an application that allows users to easily share recommendations and ratings with friends.

The solution:

Create a application with a distinct brand identity where entertainment lovers can catalog their watchlist, share reviews, and find what to watch next.

Role:

UX Designer

Research

Surveys, comparative analysis, and user interviews were conducted in order to identify and define the problem and existing solutions. This will help determine what the users’ need and/or wanting.

A survey was crafted and distributed to 35 participants via Google Forms to get a basic understanding of user media consumption habits. The ultimate goal was to further define and identify the scope of the “problem” and see if there was a need and/or interest in an application that would aid in tracking and sharing media interest. Below are the results.

When participants were asked about pain points they expereince when trying to find a new show, it all centered around “the decision making process in general”, as one user put it. Results also showed that mood (85%) and recommendations from famiy and friends (80%) were the two main factors that played into the decision making process.

Now that I had a gauged users’ pain points, interest level, and media consumption habits-it was time to scope out what solutions already existed.

A notable finding from the analysis was, that most of these applications had a social aspect to them. However, it was not the primary focus or function. This was crucial insight since my previous research revealed that 71% of users were interested in sharing recommendations with friends. And 80% relied on recommendations as the primary factor in their decision making.

During the user interviews, I had participants review the app Goodreads. This helped me scope out features and UI patterns users would like to see in a similar application.

With all the research synthesized, it was time to craft a user persona. This is an essiential I would be referring back to the persona throughout my design.

Mapping and Flows

With this being an end-to-end application, I needed to determine the information architecture and what pages I would need for an MVP. Therefore, site maps and user flows were created.

Referring back to my research, I knew that this application had emphasize the social network aspect. Due to this, I referenced social media platforms and came up with site map that resembeld what the end users already use and know.

Now having a sound grasp on what pages were needed for an MVP, I needed to establish user task flows. With this being a social app, I needed to uncover the different interactions users could take to ulitmately feed into their social feed.

Ideating

With one of the business goals being establishing a brand identity, I needed to determine brand colors, typography, and the overall look and feel of the application. This led to lo-fi wireframing and a UI kit.

For the lo-fi wireframing, I wanted to create the four main screens of the application. I primarily pulled inspiration from Pinterest, Instagram, Goodreads, and Likewise. This also helped me start the build of the UI kit.

Once i had the basis of what I wanted the app to look like-I had to create the brand identity. That meant naming the brand I was designing. I grabbed my skecthpad and fivorously starting jotting down ideas and sketches. Then I made lo-fi mockups of the ideas.

After making the mockups, I still struggled with making a final decision. And when in doubt, I go back to the users and ask for their thoughts. I made a quick Google survey and asked users to vote on which name idea they liked best.

As I continued to make variations and iterations on the logo, I decided to start building out the rest of the UI kit. I poured over color choices and scoured hundreds of fonts. I knew that I wanted Watchlist to be clean, sleek, direct, and content focused. I also wanted to challenge myself and do a darker themed pattern. Below is what I came up with.

With the components laid down as foundation-I was able to start designing mid-fi mockups. I started with the four main screens; the social feed, profile view, list view and discover page.

The design was starting to come together and before moving to hi-fi, I knew I would have to do complete some user testing. This led to creating additional mid-fi screens so I could build out a prototype for users to test.

Test and Implement

A mid-fidelity prototype was built using Figma and tested with users. Based on feedback, iterations were made to make the final hi-fi wireframes.

Below is the mid-fidelity Figma prototype that was used during user testing. I recruited 23 participants from Facebook and Slack. The user testing was done using a third-party site called Maze. Participants were given four tasks to complete and were asked general questions about the overall UI. The goal was to assess the effectiveness of the design.

With 23 participants, I was able to discover undetected issues in my designs and prototype. Once I had all the results, I quantified and sythesized my data to aid in understanding what revisions would be a priority for the MVP. Below is the data chart.
