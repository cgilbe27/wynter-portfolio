Watchlist:

Creating a social media platform for entertainment lovers.

Overview:

With a surge of streaming services and media titles, people are experiencing difficulty keeping track of their watchlists. Due to endless selections, people are also struggling with deciding what to watch next. Research has shown that many rely on friends and family recommendations when deciding on what to watch. So, what if there was an app that let people catalog and share their watchlist recommendations with family and friends.

The problem:

People are having difficulty keeping track of their media consumption as well as what they should watch next. There is also a need for an app that allows entertainment lovers to share watchlists and recommendations with friends and family.

The solution:

Create an application with a distinct brand identity where entertainment lovers can catalog and share their watchlists recommendations with others.

Role:

UX Designer

Research

Create an application with a distinct brand identity where entertainment lovers can catalog and share their watchlists recommendations with others.

A survey was crafted and distributed to 35 participants via Google Forms to get a basic understanding of user media consumption habits. The two goals were to identify and define the problem and gauge user interest.

Reported pain points from participants centered around “the decision-making process in general.”Results also showed that mood (85%) and recommendations from friends and family (80%) were the two main factors in the decision-making process.

Now that I knew users’ pain points, interest level, and media consumption habits-it was time to scope out what exisiting solutions.

A notable finding from the analysis was that most of these applications had a social aspect to them. However, it was not the primary focus or function. This finding led to what my unique selling proposition (USP) would be for Watchlist.

During the user interviews, I had participants review the app Goodreads. Participants' feedback helped me scope out features and UI patterns users would like to see in a similar application.

With all the research synthesized, it was time to craft a user persona. I would be referring back to Emily Wilson throughout my design.

Emily’s goal of sharing ratings/reviews with friends and family and one of her key motivators being recommendations- I knew that I had to focus on the social aspect of this design.

Mapping and Flows

With this being an end-to-end application, I needed to determine the information architecture and what pages I would need for an MVP. Therefore, I created a site map and a user flow.

Referring back to my research, I knew that this application had to emphasize the social network aspect. Due to this, I referenced social media platforms and came up with a site map that resembled what the end-users already use and know.

Now having a sound grasp on what pages were needed for an MVP, I needed to create a user task flow. I had to uncover the different interactions users could take to add a media title to their watchlist and lead to posting on their social feed.

Ideating

With one of the business goals being to establish a brand identity, I needed to determine brand colors, typography, and the overall look and feel of the application. This led to lo-fi wireframing and a UI kit.

For the lo-fi wireframing, I wanted to create the four main screens of the application. I primarily pulled inspiration from Pinterest, Instagram, Goodreads, and Likewise. Lo-fi wireframing helped me start the build of the UI kit.

Once I had the basis of what I wanted the app to look like, I need to name the brand I was designing. I grabbed my sketch pad started jotting down ideas and sketches. Then I made the mockups below.

After making the mockups, I still struggled with making a final decision. And when in doubt, I go back to the users and ask for their thoughts. I made a quick Google survey and asked users to vote on which name idea they liked best.

As I continued to make variations and iterations on the logo, I decided to build out the rest of the UI kit. This led to pouring over color choices and scouring hundreds of fonts. I knew that I wanted Watchlist to be clean, cool, direct, and sleek.

Test and Implement

A prototype was built using Figma and tested with users. Based on user testing results, iterations were made to the wireframes.

With the help of user and peer feedback, I was able to finalize my hi-fi screens. Below are a few device mockups of the final designs.

The social feed is the landing screen. Users could navigate to friends’ profile pages and view detailed info of the media titles displayed in posts (by clicking on the title or thumbnail). The CTA allows users to add media titles to their watchlists.
In the previous mockup, the CTA had a drop-down function. The results from user testing found the original CTA “unexpected” and “slightly confusing”. I simplified CTA and added a simple banner for a better user experience.
When navigating to a profile, the users would see recent activity. Similar to other social media platforms. Whether that activity is rating a media title, adding a friend, or adding a media title to a watchlist.
Using the tab, users could view watchlists. Research suggested having preset curated lists (watched, currently watching, and want to watch). Users would also have the ability to create their own list.
This screen is the view within a watchlist category. Users would have the ability to filter within the list as well as edit their listings. If the user viewing this list is not the user of the profile-the primary CTA “add to watchlist” would be present.
This screen is the detailed media info. In the previous design, the screen lacked a review section. Nor was there a banner and the navbar was present. Changes were made to include reviews, a banner, and exclude the navbar.

Key Takeaways

When working on a problem with existing solutions, figuring out a USP is essential. I was able to identify the USP by researching existing solutions and conducting an analysis.

When figuring out what screens are necessary for an MVP, refer to site maps and user flows. With that said, do not undervalue the importance of site maps and/or user flows. Not only do they aid in understanding user interaction but in prioritization too.
