DesignLab:

Creating a feature that allows for student-to-student interaction

Overview:

DesignLab (DL) was founded in 2013 and is a mentor-led, web design boot camp. Their goal: to empower creators to do what they love. Currently, they have 9,000+ students enrolled with over an astounding 300,000+ project submissions. The project-based curriculum includes 1:1 mentoring, group crits, and growing peer support from the student community.

The problem:

Due to Designlab being a virtual education platform (with 9,000+ students) and the added complication of Covid, students need more peer interaction and support. Students have reported struggling with a lack of peer social interaction.

The solution:

Design a feature that addresses the need for more student-to-student interaction.

Role:

UX Designer

Research

Open-ended interviews were conducted with DesignLab students to identify and define the problem. Afterward, a survey was created to assess and quantify the need.

For this project, the goal was to create a useful feature within the student portal. The original idea was to integrate DesignLab's Youtube tutorials for convenience to the students. But, I was coming up with a solution to a "problem" that I was uncertain needed solving.

Taking a few steps back, I arranged open-ended interviews with other students. To better visualize the information retained from the interviews, I created an empathy map.

It became abundantly clear that students' biggest pain point as virtual students was the lack of social interaction. Students reported feeling alone, anxious, and isolated.

At this point, I had started to identify the problem. But further research was necessary to define the problem and the scope. I created surveys to quantify and prove the need for a solution.

Just as the open-ended interviews suggested, 60% of students were less than satisfied with the amount of student-student interaction they were getting. Within the survey, students voted for a virtual study room feature that would allow them to collaborate and cowork.

Now that I knew what I would be designing- it was time to analyze existing third-party solutions.

Mapping and Flows

After research, I need to create a site map and a user flow. This would determine where the feature would exist and how students would interact with said feature.

Though this deliverable may seem simplistic, it was still necessary to figure out where the feature would exist within the portal. How would users navigate to their new virtual study rooms?

I also needed to start thinking about how users would interact with the feature. What steps would users need to take or expect to take? How can I simplify the use of this feature for easy student-to-student interaction?

Ideating

With a better understanding of the overall flow of the feature, I started to draft ideas. I sketched out multiple versions and wireframed multiple versions.

After research, I had multiple ideas and variations of the soon-to-be feature in mind. So, I grabbed a pencil and my trusty sketchpad and started drafting my ideas.

After sketching some variations, it was time to execute some mid-fi wireframes using Figma. The goal was to nail down the format and the content.

Now that I had wireframes to work with, I needed to present and get more user feedback. With the open-ended interviews being so insightful, I decided to go full-circle and do it again. With a comprised list of what I wanted to assess, it was time to schedule the interviews.

Test and Implement

A mid-fidelity prototype was built using Figma and tested with users. Based on feedback, iterations were completed to make the final hi-fi wireframes.

My goal of the open-ended interviews was to get the end-users initial feedback and assess certain design features. After receiving feedback from users, I comprised and organized the findings and created a priority matrix. This helped me visualize what was a priority.

Now, it was time to execute. I implemented feedback that I received and iterated my mid-fi wireframes. After completing the final revisions, I annotated the screens for further clarity and presentation purposes.

With the design finalized, it only felt right for me to make a working prototype. The prototype shows how users would interact with the new feature.

Key Takeaways

Do not get ahead of yourself! I made a noob move and had a “solution” before identifying and defining the problem. Research proved that there was a more blatant pain point with virtual students. Case in point, identify and define the problem before coming up with “solutions”.

Feature creep is real and, I fell victim to it in this project. If I could change one thing about my process, it would have done something like dot-voting during my ideating phase. This type of user feedback would have helped me better prioritize additional features.

Immediately after my ideating phase, I attempted A/B testing on variations of my main screen. This testing told me that users prefer a “join” CTA button. Not all research has to be super intensive to uncover something insightful.
