DesignLab: Creating a feature

A feature that allows for more student-to-student interaction

Overview:

DesignLab (DL) was founded in 2013 and is a mentor-led, web design bootcamp. Their goal: to empower creators to do what they love. Currently, they have 9,000+ students enrolled with over an astounding 300,000+ project submissions. The project-based curriculum includes 1:1 mentoring, group crits, and growing peer support from the student community.

The problem:

Due to Designlab being a virtual education platform , with 9,000+ students, and the added complication of Covid; students’ are needing more peer interaction and support. Students have reported struggling with lack of peer social interaction.

The solution:

Design a feature that addresses the need for more student-to-student interaction.

Role:

UX Designer

Research

Open-ended interviews were done with DesignLab students to idenitfy and define the probelm. Surveys were then created to assess and quantify the need.

The project that was given to me was to “add a feature” to an existing site. Immediately, I came up with solutions that addressed pain points that I expereinced as a DL student. Which was the lack of video tutorial integration within the student portal.

However, I immediately ran into an issue most UX designers face-I was coming up with a solution to a problem I was not sure existed. I had to take a couple steps back. What better way than to connect with other students and have open-ended interviews.

After just two interviews, there were already patterns forming and bigger pain points to address. To better visualize the information retained from the interviews; I created an empathy map.

It became abundantly clear that students biggest pain point as a virtual student was the lack of social interaction. Students reported feeling alone, anxious, and isolated.

At this point, I had started to idenitfy the problem but further research was necessary to define the problem. I then created surveys to quanitfy and prove the need for a solution. I even had students offer up their own solutions and vote on possible features.

Just as the open-ended interviews suggested; 60% of students were less than satisfied with the amount of student-student interaction they were getting. And the votes were in-students voted for a virtual study room feature that would allow for collaborating and coworking.

Now that I knew what I would be designing- it was time analyze existing third-party solutions. How do they structure their UI? What features are available to the users? What are thier successes and pitfalls?

Mapping and Flows

To figure out where the feature would exist on the site and how the users would interact with the feature; a site map and user flow was created.

Though this deliverable may seem simplistic in nature (because it is), it was still important to understand where the feature would exist within the site. How would users navigate to their new virtual study rooms?

I also needed to start thinking about how users would interact with the feature. What steps would users need to take...or expect to take? How can I simplify the use of this feature? Below is the user flow I created in order to better understand the overall flow of the feature.

Ideating

With a better understanding of the overall flow of the feature, I started to draft ideas. From sketches to mid and hifi wireframing; multiple versions were created.

After research, I had multiple ideas and variations of the soon-to-be feature in mind. So, I grabbed a pencil and my trusty sketchpad and started sketching.

After sketching some variations, it was time to execute some mid-fi wireframes using Figma. The goal was to nail down the format, the content and what additional features would be necessary to meet users expectations.

Now that I had wireframes to work with, I needed to present and get more user feedback. With the open-ended interviews in the beginning being so insightful; I decided to to go full-circle and do it again. With a comprised list of what I wanted to assess, it was time to schedule the interviews.

Test and Implement

Open-ended interviews were done to assess certain features and overall flow. Based on feedback, a priority matrix was made and iterations were done accordingly.

My goal of the open-ended interviews was to get the end users initial thoughts and impressions as well as assess certain aspects of the site. For example, I wanted to know if the “Study stats” feature would be useful and/or delightful.

After recieving all feedback of users, I comprised and organzied the findings and created a priority matrix. This really helped me visualize what was high priority in terms of what iterations needed to be done to finialize my hi-fi wireframes.

Now, it was time to execute. I implemented feedback that I recieved and iterated my mid-fi wireframes. After completing the final revisions, I annotated the screens for further clarity and for presentation purposes.

With the design finalized, it only felt right for me to make a working prototype. The prototype was built to visually show how users would interact with the new feature.

Key Takeaways

Do not get ahead of yourself! I made noob move as a UX designer and came up with a “solution” before identifying and defining the problem. I thought with me being one of the end-users, I was fully aware of all the pain points us virtual students face. However, research proved me wrong in the best way possible and I was able to uncover a bigger, more blatant pain point. Which led me to identifying the problem of students not being satisfied with their current amount of student-to-student interaction. Case in point, identify and define the problem before coming up with “solutions”.

Feature creep is real and I definitely became a victim of it in this project. The task was to add a feature to an existing site and from the moment I started sketching; I was adding features within features without any research backing the need. If I could change one thing about my process, it would have done something like dot-voting during my ideating phase. This type of user feedback would have helped me better prioritize additional features.

Not all research uncovers something super insightful. Immediately after my ideating phase, I attempted A/B testing on varitions of my main screen. Though this testing told me that users prefer a “join” CTA button, it did not tell me much else. And that is okay! Little user insight is better than no user insight .
