Collectibles & Games:

Designing a Responsive Ecommerce Site

Overview:

Collectible and Games (CG) is a newly established small business that specializes in selling Yu-Gi-OH cards. They previously sold using a third-party marketplace site but with an increase of sales, the stakeholders wanted to create their own responsive, ecommerce site.

Though CG specializes in Yu-Gi-OH, they have a five year plan where the goal is to expand product inventory outside of Yu-Gi-Oh. This beings said, they wanted a site that would be easily adaptable for future expansion.

The problem:

CG is unable to control their product cost due to using a third-party marketplace site. Ultimately, negatively impacting revenue and business growth.

The solution:

Create a responsive e-commerce site and begin establishing a brand identity.

Role: UX Designer

Research

Stakeholder Interviews, Competitor Analysis and User Interviews were done to better understand the probelm, the business goals and to uncover possible solutions.

In order to understand the business and the business goals, I conducted interviews with the stakeholders. We started by idenitfying the problem in which they are attempting to solve and solutions.

Create a responsive, ecommerce site to cutout the “middle-man”.

Begin establishing a brand identity for Collectibles and Games

Design a site that would be easily adapatable for future expansion.

After getting a clearer picture of the business and the stakeholders’ goals-it was time to initiate a competior analysis.

The competitor analysis was helpful BUT I needed to know the user. What do the users like? What do they dislike? What are their needs and how can we meet them? After conducting interviews with avid Yu-Gi-Oh duelist; I created a persona which honed in on users’ goals and pain points.

Mapping and Flows

Using the information I gathered through research, I drafted a product roadmap. I then created a site map and a user flow of the checkout process.

With a pressing timeline, it was essiential to know what pages to design in order to deliver what would be the MVP.

Now knowing what pages were high priority, I moved forward with making a site map and user flow for better clarity of the information architecture of the site.

Ideating

With one of the business goals being establishing a brand identity which required me to brainstorm logo ideas. I also started to sketch and build out lofi wireframes.

With the brand planning on future expansion, I explored a multitude of ideas ranging from wordmarks to pixel art. Below are a few drafted ideas.

While continuing to collaborate with the stakeholders to nail down their brand vision, I began working on lofi wireframing. We knew the site was going to be product heavy, therefore, product focused. With that in mind, I sketched out some lofi mobile wireframes.

After sketching some variations, it was time to execute some wireframes using Figma. The goal was to get the bare bones for the site laid out. How, what, and where the content would fit and be presented.

Test and Implement

A mid-fidelity prototype was built using Figma and tested with users. Based on feedback, iterations were made to make the final hi-fi wireframes.

Below is the mid-fidelity Figma prototype that was used during user testing. Users were given the prompt to find a single, monster effect card and add it to the cart. The goal was to test the overall flow, the ease of filtering, and general Yu-Gi-OH specific content.

After completing user testing; I reviewed notes, quanitifed where I could, and grouped feedback accordingly. This helped prioritize the revisions that needed to be made.

With user feedback in hand, I sat down with the stakeholders and addressed some concerns and possible solutions. From there, I finished iterations in order to deliver the final MVP.

Key Takeaways

Visions change and that is okay! With CG being a new business, there was quite a bit of back and forth on what we wanted the look and the feel of the site to be. But we all agreed with the given timeline producing a MVP was the most important. Iterations will continue to be made as the brand continues to evolve and expand.

With a short project deadline, I should have appropriately allocated more of my time to the actual design. For example, I did not anticipate how long the filtering system would take to design. Looking back, during the research stage, I would have asked the users more questions centered around filtering. This would have given me a better sense of direction and better allocation of my time.
