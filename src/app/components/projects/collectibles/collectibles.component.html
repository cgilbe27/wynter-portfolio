Collectibles & Games:

Designing a Responsive Ecommerce Site

Overview:

Collectible and Games (CG) is a newly established small business that specializes in selling Yu-Gi-Oh cards. They previously sold using a third-party marketplace site. But with an increase in sales, the stakeholders wanted to have their own responsive e-commerce site.

Though CG specializes in Yu-Gi-Oh, they have a five-year plan where the goal is to expand product inventory outside of Yu-Gi-Oh. With this said, they wanted a site that would be easily adaptable for future expansion.

The problem:

CG is unable to control its product cost due to using a third-party marketplace site. Ultimately negatively impacting revenue and business growth.

The solution:

Create a responsive e-commerce site and begin establishing a brand identity.

Role:

UX Designer

Research

Stakeholder interviews, competitor analysis, and user interviews were conducted. These were done to better understand the business goals, the problem, and possible solutions.

To understand the business and the business goals, I conducted interviews with the stakeholders. I needed to get a better grasp of what the product they would be selling, why they no longer wanted to use a third-party site, and what they envisioned for the brand.

Create a responsive, ecommerce site to cutout the “middle-man”.

After getting a clearer picture of the business and the stakeholders’ goals, it was time to initiate a competitor analysis. I analyzed the UI, the features, and the products offered.

Now that I had a better understanding of the product and our competitors, I wanted to further empathize with the users. I interviewed Yu-Gi-Oh hobbyists. I questioned their buying process, their goals, and their pain points. After the research, I crafted Drew for our user persona.

Users are concerned with ensuring quality, getting the best price, and buying from a trusted seller. During the interviews, I was surprised by the amount of research users do before making a purchase. These users do not just view Yu-Gi-Oh as a card game but as an investment.

Mapping and Flows

Using the information I gathered through research, I drafted a product roadmap. I then created a site map and a user flow of the checkout process.

Using the MoSCoW method, I was able to determine what pages were a must-have to deliver an MVP. The must-have pages included a landing page, the product page, and a filtering system specific to Yu-Gi-Oh.

With knowing what pages were a priority, I was able to make a site map. The site map helped establish the information architecture of the site.

I found it necessary to create a user flow centered around the checkout process due to this being an e-commerce site. I thought outside of the MVP and included nodes that referenced future design ideas gathered from research. I.e. the ability to request a specific product.

Ideating

To start establishing a brand identity, I started logo creation, choosing brand colors, and typography. I also started to sketch and build out lo-fi wireframes.

With the brand planning on future expansion, I explored a multitude of ideas ranging from wordmarks to pixel art. Below are a few drafted ideas.

While continuing to collaborate with the stakeholders to nail down their brand vision, I began working on lo-fi wireframing. We knew the site was going to be product heavy, therefore, product-focused. With that in mind, I sketched out some lo-fi mobile wireframes.

After sketching some variations, it was time to execute some wireframes using Figma. The goal was to get the bare bones for the site laid out. How, what, and where the content would fit and look on the site.

Test and Implement

A mid-fidelity prototype was built using Figma and tested with users. Based on feedback, iterations were made to make the final hi-fi wireframes.

Below is the Figma prototype that was used during user testing. Users were prompted to find a single, monster effect card and add it to the cart. The goal was to test the effectiveness of the design, the overall flow, and the ease of filtering.

After completing user testing: I reviewed notes, quantified where I could, and grouped feedback accordingly. The testing helped prioritize the revisions needed for the final designs.

With user feedback in hand, I was able to sit down with the stakeholders to discuss concerns raised from testing and possible solutions. From there, I was able to finish the iterations necessary for the final MVP.

Key Takeaways

Visions change and that is okay! But we all agreed with the given timeline producing an MVP was the most important. Iterations will continue as the brand continues to evolve and expand.

With the project timeline, I should have appropriately allocated more of my time to the actual design. For example, I did not anticipate how long the filtering system would take to design. Looking back, I would have asked the users more questions centered around filtering.
